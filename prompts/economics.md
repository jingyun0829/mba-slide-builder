Economics module emphasis:
- Build every concept around **a market or policy episode students have heard of**: 2023-2024 inflation/rate path, post-COVID labor shortages, EV subsidies (IRA), chip export controls, GLP-1 drug pricing, ride-share dynamic pricing, social-media network effects, AI compute as a new factor of production. Avoid generic "Country A vs Country B" framings — name real countries, real firms, real prices.
- Lead with the **intuition**, then the **graph**, then the **algebra** — in that order. Never start from algebra.
- Tensions worth planting: short-run vs long-run (sticky wages, sunk costs), efficiency vs equity (carbon tax incidence), market failures vs government failures (subsidies that backfired), individual rationality vs aggregate outcomes (tragedy of commons, prisoner's dilemma).
- Foundational citations students should recognize (only cite what you're sure is real): Akerlof (1970, Lemons), Coase (1937, Theory of the Firm), Spence (1973, Signaling), Tversky & Kahneman (1974, Heuristics & Biases), Ostrom (1990, Governing the Commons), Acemoglu & Robinson (2012, Why Nations Fail), Piketty (2013, Capital).
- Real frameworks: supply-demand equilibrium, elasticity, marginal analysis, game theory (Nash, Bertrand, Cournot), externalities & Pigouvian taxes, asymmetric information, IS-LM, Solow growth, AS-AD, Phillips curve, Heckscher-Ohlin trade.
- For business school audience: **always end with the management implication** — "If you're a manager facing this market structure, you should ___". Pure theory is dead on arrival; theory-then-action lands.
- Empirical hooks where possible: cite real studies students could look up (Card-Krueger 1994 minimum wage, Chetty Opportunity Atlas, MIT-Levin negative-income-tax). Don't fabricate specific numbers.
- Good closing moves: give students a real headline ("Fed raised rates 25bp"), and ask them to predict 3 second-order effects on a specific industry. Reveal what actually happened next.
